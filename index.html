Painter / Visual Artist

My work explores psychic pressure, fractured identity, and the uneasy relationship between the body and systems of occupation. Through distorted figures, geometric interruption, and saturated color fields, I construct images of tension, dislocation, and resistance.

Practice

My practice centers on painting as a psychological and spatial field. I use the body not as a stable subject, but as a site of pressure, distortion, and transformation. Fragments of limbs, displaced organs, blocked gestures, and geometric divisions recur throughout the work, forming a visual language of internal conflict and external control.

Rather than illustrating a narrative, I build compressed emotional situations. The paintings move between figuration and abstraction, intimacy and violence, stillness and interruption. What appears in them is not simply a body, but a body being reorganized by fear, discipline, memory, and invisible systems of force.

Statement

My recent work investigates the condition of being occupied psychologically, socially, and spatially. I am interested in the moment when a person no longer feels fully aligned with their own body, thoughts, or desires. In these paintings, the figure becomes unstable: ears are removed, limbs are reassigned, organs are displaced, gestures are blocked, and space is cut into artificial structures.

These distortions are not surreal effects for their own sake. They are ways of speaking about pressure: the pressure to adapt, to perform, to obey, to remain functional, and to survive within systems that quietly reorganize human experience. The body in my work is often compressed, interrupted, or segmented because it carries the trace of that occupation.

Color plays a crucial role in this process. I use saturated, often seductive color fields against scenes of discomfort, fragmentation, or unease. This tension allows beauty and violence to coexist within the same image. I want the viewer to feel both attraction and resistance: to enter the work visually, but remain unsettled psychologically.

Painting, for me, is a way to make inner conflict visible. It is also a way to resist smoothness, coherence, and the illusion of control. What I seek is not a resolved image, but a charged one: an image in which freedom appears threatened, deformed, and still somehow present.

Works

The works are organized as evolving fragments of a larger inquiry into deformation, occupation, and the unstable condition of human freedom. Each series approaches the body as a contested space: cut, displaced, restrained, or reassembled.
